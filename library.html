Library

I do almost all my reading on Kindle, so I don't have a bookshelf to gaze fondly upon. So instead, this page is my virtual bookshelf.

I list only books I have actually finished, or am currently reading. They are in reverse chronological order, with the most recently completed at the top.

Pieces of the Action[in progress]

Vannevar Bush

Immune[in progress]

Philipp Dettmer

The Wind's Twelve Quarters[in progress]

Ursula K. Le Guin

Algorithms to Live By

Brian Christian

Completed: Nov

A fun and highly readable about how algorithms manifest in real life.

Oceanic

Greg Egan

Completed: Nov

Another collection of hard sci-fi short stories - although many of them are not very short (almost novellas). Not my favorite by Egan, but enjoyable.

Lost in Math

Sabine Hossenfelder

Completed: Oct

I had this book in my "to-read" backlog for a long time. I hesitated because I've mostly lost interest in physics, despite having done a degree in it long ago. But this book far exceeded expectations - it's a fun and engaging read, and I powered through it quite quickly. I think I liked it because it articulates much of the disillusionment that caused me to lose interest in physics in the first place.

Unsettled

Steven E. Koonin

Completed: Oct

A very well-written engaging journey through the science of climate change. A great counter to the alarmism that's rampant in the media.

The Cold Start Problem

Andrew Chen

Completed: Oct

Pretty much a series of shallow case-studies of a bunch of tech companies, trying to tie some of their successes (or failures) to the concept of the network effect.

A bit longer than needed, in my opinion; it didn't really hold my interest, and it took my months of off-an-on reading to get through it.

Deep Learning from Scratch

Seth Weidman

Completed: Oct

I loved this book! An exceptionally well structured and thorough introduction to neural networks by building everything from scratch.

I read it start to finish as a first pass, and an now doing a second pass through, implmenting everything myself.

The Philosophy Gym

Stephen Law

Completed: August

A good snack-book; that is, it's easy to read in snack-sized portions, as each chapter is independent and fairly short.

I'm not sure I learned a whole lot about philosophy, other than "pilisophers are weird", but I already knew that. Some of the chapters were thought-provoking. Others were just silly. But overall worth a read.

Norwegian Wood

Haruki Murakami

Completed: July

Brilliant. Easily one of the best novels I've read, ever. Read it. Don't rush through it. Savor.

Soonish

Kelly & Zach Weinersmith

Completed: July

A great overview of some possible future technologies. Lots of humor makes it a fun read. Very similar in style to Randall Munroe's books.

The Chemist

Stephenie Meyer

Completed: July

Well written and fun, if a bit cheesy in parts. Sort-of Twilight with spies instead of vampires (or so I guess, I never read Twilight).

Dark Pools

Scott Patterson

Completed: July

This one had been in my queue for a long time, and I finally read it during vacation this year.

A fascinating story about the origins of high-speed trading, with a focus a few of the key players. I had not realized how recently the stock markets had become computerized. Kind-of got me inspired to get into automated trading (although I'm not sure that's the take-away that the author intended).

The Hidden Girl and Other Stories

Ken Liu

Completed: June

Another very good collection on sci-fi short stories. Not quite as masterful as Ted Chiang, but very good.

Stories of Your Life and Others

Ted Chiang

Completed: June

Ted Chiang is far and away the best short story writer working today. This, like his other collection Exhalation, is in another league.

Map and Territory

Eliezer Yudkowsky

Completed: June

Really more a collection of blog posts than a book. Mostly about cognitive biases. Ends with a bizarrely long and tedious sci-fi story that I just couldn't get through, but otherwise not bad.

Between Two Kingdoms

Suleika Jaouad

Completed: May

Excellent. Autobiographical account of the author's life with cancer. Very human and moving.

The Alignment Problem

Brian Christian

Completed: May

Good book, although not what I expected. A historical overview of issues with making ML systems do what we want them to in a way that we can trust and understand. Not at all about the future speculative AI catastrophe that some are predicting these days (for that, see Life 3.0 by Tegmark, or Superintelligence by Bostrom).

Our Man in Havana

Graham Greene

Completed: April

Good story. A bit cheesy, and I didn't love it as much as The End of The Affair, but well written. I'm sure I'll read more by Greene.

Termination Shock

Neal Stephenson

Completed: April

Long, entertaining. Not my favorite by Stephenson (so far that's Seveneves), but I enjoyed it.

Night

Elie Wiesel

Completed: April

I think this is one of the books to re-read every few years. This was my second time. Haunting, disturbing, classic.

Arnold: The Education of a Bodybuilder

Arnold Schwarzenegger

Completed: March

The first part is biographical, recounting Arnold's start in bodybuilding, up until his domination of the sport. It's striking how utterly obsessed (and, less charitably, narcissistic) he is (or was). The second half is instructional, and really could have been a separate book.

The Structure of Scientific Revolutions

Thomas S. Kuhn

Completed: March

This is a classic that has been in my "to-read" backlog for years. Having finally ready it, I have to say I'm a little disappointed. This feels like an essay that was forcibly expanded into book length.

The Courage to Be Disliked

Ichiro Kishimi & Fumitake Koga

Completed: March

I read this because I saw it recommended several times. Not a fan. I guess I can see how it could be inspirational for certain personality types, but for me it just didn't resonate at all.

The Prince

Niccolò Machiavelli

Completed: Feb

I'm surprised I don't see this touted as a must-read for startup founders, the way The Art of War is. A collection of advice about how to succeed as a "Prince" (a noble, or landowner, or feudal lord), to conquer enemies, and to control and gain the love of your subjects. The second-to-last chapter alone makes the book worthwhile.

Instantiation

Greg Egan

Completed: Feb

Very thought-provoking collection of hard science-fiction stories. It's good.

Where is My Flying Car?

J. Storrs Hall

Completed: Feb

Very interesting book.

To be honest, this is not quite what I expected. I thought the "Flying Car" in the title was mostly symbolic, and the book would be more about general technological progress. In fact, a pretty large part of the book is literally about Flying Cars (or, more accurately, small personal aircraft), which have a much richer history than I suspected.

But there are also chapters about an eclectic collection of other areas in which technology has not advanced as we once hoped it might. These range from the relatively uncontroversial like nuclear power, to stranger ones like cold-fusion (which the author thinks was dismissed too hastily), to some batshit-crazy stuff about nanotech.

Enjoyable and well worth reading, with a large grain of salt.

Numbers Don't Lie

Vaclav Smil

Completed: Jan

An easy-to-read collection of short chapters, covering a wide variety of facts about the world, from economics to science to nature to politics.

Atomic Habits

James Clear

Completed: Jan

Every few years I forget that I don't like self-help books, and get suckered into reading another self-help book. For a bad genre, this one was actually OK. Personally, prefer to just periodically watch Shia LaBeouf yelling "just do it", that's all the self-help I need.

Finite and Infinite Games

James P. Carse

Completed: Jan

If this book came out today, I would swear it was written by GPT-3 and published as a hoax. But it was written in 1986, allegedly by a human.

It has all the hallmarks of AI-generated text - the sentences are grammatically correct, and almost seem to make sense if you read just a few at a time. But any more than that, and the facade falls away; it's all just nonsense, with no perceptible connection to reality.

This is probably the worst book I have ever actually read all the way to the end of. I honestly don't know why I wasted my time. The only good thing I can say about it is that it's not very long.

The Storyteller

Dave Grohl

Completed: Jan

Sort-of an autobiography, but more of a collection of stories from Dave's life. If you're hoping for an comprehensive history of Nirvana and Foo Fighters, this is not it. But if you are a fan of those bands, as I am, and want some fun annecdotes from a life in rock and roll, this book is very worthwhile.
